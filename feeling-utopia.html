3. Feeling Utopia

Post-Pandoran Depression

This chapter draws on TikTok, Reddit, and sentiment analysis to show how fans move from grief to action.

Read on

Overview

There is a phenomenon that fans know by name, which is called Post-Avatar Depression Syndrome or Post Pandoran Depression. This phenomenon is characterised by strong emotional grief felt by fans after watching the Avatar films, which occurs when they return to their current worlds which seem dimmer, demanding, and less alive. It was first introduced and explained by fans after the first film was released in 2009. This experience for many people is both real and recurring, and this is where this section begins.

As Constructing Utopia and Speaking into Being shows fans building and practicing Pandora through fanfiction, community and language learning, Feeling Utopia is deconstructing what happens in the emotional space before this. It explores the ways fans feel longing, grief and rage at their own current world they live in, and if fans stay in this painful state or move to something different. The data here comes from various Tiktok posts and Reddit posts, as well as a sentiment analysis comparing Avatar and StarTrek fan communities. What I found was the most emotionally urgent story to tell yet.


So what does it feel like to long for a world that doesn't exist?


The same coding framework used in Speaking into Being and Constructing Utopia, will be used here throughout. EB codes (Extending Beyond the Film) track what fans are doing creatively and expressively, while UD codes (Utopian Desire) track what fans are longing for that are represented in the film and are missing from our own world. However, UD codes are doing something more here, as they are not being constructed by fans but rather being felt as something missing or lost within their current world, and in some cases transformed into action in the current world.



A world that was supposed to be different

To understand what Avatar fans are experiencing it is both helpful and necessary to step back and ask what kind of world Avatar fans are living in? Sociologist Émile Durkheim first developed the term anomie in 1893, which he explained was the feeling of being disconnected and in a state of "normlessness" in modern society when strong community ties, shared meaning, and a sense of belonging break down due to fast social and economic change (Durkheim, 1893/2014). At the end of the 19th century, Durkheim argued that the absence of strong community structures leaves individuals lost, without a shared meaning or sense of belonging to something larger than themselves (Durkheim, 1893/2014, p. 48). He argues that without these connections, society starts to break down and individuals begin to suffer (Durkheim, 1893/2014).

Durkheim's anomie still holds significant meaning today. Now, of course, though Durkheim could not have possibly anticipated climate change, social media, industrial farming, or late stage capitalism, his idea of anomie maps with uncomfortable precision onto the world that many Avatar fans describe. Post-Pandoran Depression is about anomie recognizing its own cure. Pandora offers everything that modern life has structurally removed such as, genuine community, meaningful purpose, ecological connection, spiritual continuity, and grief held collectively rather than hidden. When fans leave this world at the end of the film concludes and go out to a world filled with loud traffic, construction, screens, and disconnection, they are feeling not just the sadness about a fictional world but rather a distance between what human life could be and what it currently is.

As a Gen Z researcher shaped by ecological anxiety, a global pandemic, and the exhaustion of growing up in a world that seems to be in constant crisis, I find that these feelings expressed by the Avatar fandom resonate strongly with my generation and earlier ones. Prescott and Shank (2025) say ecological utopias produce stronger motivation to change society than others, because they produce feelings of the loss of something rather than just the possibility of a world better than our own. These utopias make us feel something we were supposed to have but can't, such as land that is protected and sacred, communities that are unconditional, and life having purpose beyond productivity. For a generation already feeling burned out before even starting their careers, this part of Pandora's appeal is quite powerful, as it doesn't just imagine a better world, but a better reason to live in it.



The cruel loop

Post-Pandoran Depression is not only a representation of Durkheim's anomie but also Lauren Berlant's (2011) concept of "cruel optimism." She describes this notion as the attachment to an object or ideal that fans rely on and hold onto that helps you keep going, but at the same time stops you from truly thriving. The very thing you long for becomes the thing that wears you out. Post Pandoran Depression is this made visible and named by the people experiencing it.

"Post Avatar depression is real. I remember just staring off into space for hours after seeing TWOW, just wishing I could go back and watch it all over again, so I'm right there with you." The film ends. The grief begins. And then fans go back and watch it again.

This is Berlant's (2011) cruel loop in the purest form as the fan watches, grieves and returns to the film to relieve the grief, only to find that it gives more grief than it relieves. Holtmeier (2013) describes exactly this cycle of fans seeking remission through either the films' soundtrack, repeated viewing of the film, or obsessing over Pandoran landscapes. This creates what he calls an endless cycle that leads back to the same fictional world of Pandora rather than creating real change in the real world. Lauren Berlant's (2011) would recognize this as cruel optimism where this attachment to the fictional world sustains fans, but also slowly undermines their ability to truly flourish.



Naming what is missing

Importantly, Post-Pandora Depression is not only a loop, but also a form of diagnosis. This grief in this fandom is very articulated by fans. They are not simply sad; they are naming with precision what the real world is doing wrong and what Pandora represents as the perfect alternative. Zuolo (2025) calls this estrangement where the utopia makes you see your own world differently, and defamiliarises the status quo. It makes fans ask why things in the world are the way they are. The origin of the defamiliarisation concept has a fascinating history worth examining here. The Russian Formalists first proposed the concept of defamiliarisation in the 1920s, who asserted that the purpose of art was to disrupt automatic perception, and by doing so render new the familiar. Bertolt Brecht developed this idea into the "alienation effect" as an intentional performance device that would prevent audiences from blindly receiving their surroundings and have them reflect on the structure of society. This same vein of thought can be traced directly into the Marxian cultural analysis, where Jameson (2005) studies on utopian impulse demonstrate that even mass culture contains a critique of the social conditions under which it was created.

This fan is doing exactly what Jameson (2005) describes as utopian work. Avatar fans stating "we've bound or limited ourselves in this process" captures exactly the action created by Brecht's intent for his theatre to not only provoke an emotional response in his audience but also cause them to observe their world as a construction that could be transformed. They are reading the real world for clues about what is missing, using Pandora as the lens. UD3 here is not just a personal preference for nature, but is a structural critique. Fans are acknowledging that we built the wrong type of society, and Avatar is where that awareness turns into a strong emotional feeling. Durkheim's (1893/2014) anomie is present here in every line as this fan shows clearly that modern systems have severed something essential, which was not inevitable.

This TikTok is a direct list of complaints about the modern world. Every single reason given for loving the Na'vi is in direct opposition to something specific in the world fans are living in in real life. These include, exploitation of bodies, work strictly as production rather than purpose, inequality, and finally environmental destruction. This fan used Zuolo (2025) estrangement as well here, using Pandora to make the world which we see as normal and familiar as strange and immoral. The comment to this post by another user who notes, "this is how most countries were living before colonisation" shows the connection fans are beginning to make about the film to real world political and historical realities.



The colonial thread

That colonial perspective is not an isolated observation. Many fans across the Tiktok and Reddit data repeatedly and deliberately map the colonial narrative of the film to the real world colonialist issues. This simply emerges from the emotional logic of the longing.

This casual comment written on TikTok is actually doing something remarkable. Fans are able to map the colonial extractive corporation in the film to the history of this exact thing on Earth. This is exactly the cosmopolitical function Adamson (2012) describes, where Avatar unveils the culture-nature divide and the colonial systems that created it. This fan most likely did not read Adamson (2012), yet they arrived at the same conclusion through feeling. This is such a powerful moment in the data, as it shows fan communities organically acknowledging political and historical contexts within the film through the emotional logic of longing.

These fans are not seeing Pandora as a separate planet anymore, they are seeing it as what Earth would be if it was treated right.



From depression to action

As we've learned Berlant's (2011) cruel optimism would suggest that fans would continue in a cycle of grief, remission and more grief again. However, in some cases, this fandom does something different. While some fans in this fandom do find themselves in this never ending despair cycle, some interestingly turn the grief outward.


r/Avatar — Post-Avatar Depression thread

"It's not abnormal or stupid to yearn for something shown in fiction — especially when in our own history, not that long ago, we lived that way as well. I get it, it takes energy and time we don't all have, but you've gotta take action too. Just like the Na'vi, everyone has a place in its community, you've just gotta find it and make it comfortable. Try a new hobby or a new thing. You can grow seeds in your small apartment. You can build things with your hands with recycled materials. You can see the rain as a blessing for growth instead of being wet. However, do reach out. Please, don't suffer in silence. You are not alone." The community holds the grief, and then points it somewhere.

This post does three really important things all at once. This fan validates the feelings of other fans who are experiencing Post-Pandoran Depression which reassures that the longing others are feeling is real. They give historical context suggesting that the Na'vi way of living existed before and is not just merely a fantasy, and also redirect these feelings into real world action. They offer new ways to apply Na'vi values into real life. This directly reflects Zuolo's (2025) idea of galvanisation where a utopia inspires action in the real world instead of just existing in a state of sadness and distance. UD5 here is not just imagined solely in Pandora, but it is being practiced in real time through these fans' interactions with each other. This connects to Durkheim through the way these fan communities act as "secondary communities" where they create meaningful social bonds, which helps the individuals integrate into shared social life (Durkheim, 1893/2014, p. 48).

This post moves the argument from grief to action in a way that is direct and deliberate. The fan is not mourning Pandora, they are extracting from it. The language of "lessons," "wisdom," and "saving our world" frames Pandora not as a fictional escape but as a source of ethical guidance for real life. EB8 is doing significant work here, as the fan is explicitly connecting the values of the Na'vi to the real world and calling for their application. This connects again to Holtmeier's (2013) Na'vi sympathy, as the attachment to Pandora prompts action in the social and material environments of our world rather than recursive return to the film. It is also what Zuolo (2025) calls galvanisation, where a utopia functions not just as critique but as motivation.



Two utopias, two emotional systems

Not all sci-fi fandoms imagine and long for the same kind of better world. To fully understand Avatar's unique utopian vision and why it produces a particular grief and transformation, I compared Avatar and Star Trek fan discourse. For this comparison I used sentiment analysis and thematic coding of similar Reddit Threads from both communities that focused on longing and appreciating their respective fictional worlds.

As both show some neutral and approving sentiment, beneath the surface, their emotional profiles differ sharply.


See for yourself…


As Prescott and Shank (2025) found that ecological utopias produce stronger motivational responses than technological ones because they evoke loss rather than aspiration, this data shows exactly that distinction. Star Trek fandoms say: we can get there, while Avatar fandom says: we were already there and we lost it. This is clearly not a simple difference in fictional preference, but rather a difference in how the two fandoms imagine a better world. The Star Trek fandom believes that we are slowly moving towards a better future and thinks that society can fix its current problems through technological progress, while the Avatar fandom feels as though something deeply important has already been lost and sees the better world as only existing in the past.

This difference reflects deeper issues within the contemporary world. The Avatar fandom expresses Émile Durkheim's concept of anomie as a feeling of disconnection, meaninglessness, and lack of belonging, which is felt by fans as a deep, lasting wound. Star Trek experiences anomie as temporary and is hopeful it will be resolved through advancements and social change.

What's important here is that the emotions expressed by Avatar fans are not exactly new, but rather echo past historical anxieties. For example, during the industrial evolution people felt separated from nature and land as they moved away from the country into cities and communities based solely on productivity. These emotions were weaved into romantic literature which emphasized nature, emotion and what was lost, and early environmentalism which tried to protect what remained of nature. The Avatar fandom represented this exact grief but in a modern version. While people in the past grieved the loss of rural life, Avatar fans grief the loss of the entire planet's health. Avatar fans see Pandora as a kind of memory of a better world, which was made to feel real and familiar and then completely ripped away, making the sense of loss so painful.

Result

Taken together, the TikTok videos, Reddit posts, and the sentiment data in this section show something that the fanfiction and language data in Constructing Utopia and Speaking into Being could only gesture towards. This final data collection shows the emotional and political stakes of the Avatar fandom which are truly real, urgent and historically specific.

But why this fandom, and why now? There are a few reasons. First, many fans experience what Émile Durkheim calls anomie, which is a feeling of alienation when the social bonds that used to connect us are broken, leaving us feeling cut off from our communities and the natural environment. Today's newer generations also face a significant crisis regarding the environment. As much as we can see the damage being done to the planet, it feels like there are not enough ways to act collectively on something meaningful. This leads to people feeling frustrated and hopeless.

Traditional systems of belief have also crumbled in modern day society. Many people have left behind organized religion or other institutions that once provided meaning to their lives, and as such, many individuals experience a sense of spiritual emptiness or feel uncertain about what they believe in and where they fit in.

James Cameron's Avatar taps into this powerful message. The Na'vi have a spiritual existence not based on rigid rules or hierarchies, but grounded in their connection to Eywa, their ancestors and other living beings. This gives the Na'vi immense meaning and a strong sense of belonging. The foundation of their belief system is built upon the relationship between themselves and the earth, their lived experiences with their community, and the experiences of each other. For many people who are spiritually disconnected with their lives, Pandora provides a means of visualising this connection again.

Avatar fans are then able to create a collective imaginary world that reflects all these emotions of disconnection, environmental despair, lack of meaning, and the need for community. Online fan communities help to identify and articulate these expressions into a collective experience. This way, these fan organisations act as both support systems and belief systems, fulfilling a role that larger society has failed to provide. Therefore, Pandora is much more than a form of escape, but rather as a means of expressing the real issues of life, and sometimes motivating individuals to pursue these emotions in a real-world experience.

Berlant (2011) asks whether the attachment to an idealized object is ultimately beneficial or damaging for the people who hold it. The answer I've come to is both. Those Avatar fans who alone only passively reimmerse themselves within the media will ultimately stay in the cruel loop. But fans who find each other and help each other in online fandom communities begin to do something with the grief. They hold each other through the grief of realising what is lost and missing in the real world, and point each other towards action they can take today, making them just a little closer to the life of the Na'vi people.

The Avatar fandom does not resolve Berlant's (2011) cruel optimism, but it does move through it collectively and purposefully.
