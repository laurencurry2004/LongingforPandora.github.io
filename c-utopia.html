1. Constructing Utopia

Living in Pandora

This chapter analyses Avatar fanfiction to show how fans build Pandora through the quiet details of everyday life.

Read on

Overview

When I first started reading Avatar fanfiction, I found myself drawn to the quieter, “slice of life” stories rather than the grand adventurous or epic battle stories. Through the quiet and simple stories I read, I began to understand what Pandora was like without the dramatic or tumultuous events happening. I found that fans were not writing about the fantastic battles in the world, but rather about simple life in harmony.

As I read I decided to dive into two fanfictions that held powerful messages within them. These were So Somewhere in My Childhood and A Spider and the Complex. These fan made stories lead me to ask what creative choices fans make when they have free access to expand the world of Pandora, and why it is that they choose to do this. I began to wonder what it is that they consistently add, linger on, and what this could tell us about what Pandora means to its fans.

What became very clear throughout this process of inquiry was that Pandora seemed to feel more livable to fans than the world we actually inhabit.


So how is that sense of livability created?


I’ve used two coding frameworks that run through my analysis to explore this. EB codes (Extending Beyond the FIlm) track what fans are doing creatively as they expand, invent or repair Pandora’s world beyond what the films show us. UD codes (Utopian Desire) track what values and ways of living represented in Pandora that fans long for as it feels missing from our own world. These two codes work together not just to show how fans are building a utopia, but how they build it, and what it tells us about why they feel they need to inhibit this world.

This scene builds utopia through texture, not event. The fan describes a parent weaving, a child breathing, a sense of calm. These are the things that fans are drawn to write when they are free to write whatever they want. Fan studies scholars like Jenkins (2003) explore the way fans are active producers who create fiction so that they can shift into “unrealized possibilities” (p.g 24) as they understand and articulate the frustrations they have with the real world around them and transform these into imaginative alternatives like Pandora. What this specific scene does is repress the noise, the speed, and the disconnection that is present in modern life. What it actively reimagines instead is slowness (UD6), craft and calm presence.

This pattern repeats continuously throughout both fanfictions.

This description isn’t about the fire or the food, but rather it’s about the way Spider feels. The physical relief that he experiences from being somewhere that doesn’t require him to hold himself together is where the UD2 code operates, which is characterized by the specific safety of being allowed to be tired, soft and unguarded. What the fan is adding through EB1 is the feeling of a home that accepts you. It describes the exhaustion of living in a world that forces constant self-presentation.

Slowness in Pandora also represents a childhood where running through grass rather than sitting in classrooms is valued. It’s about valuing chasing animals rather than pursuing advancements. This is set against a world which values a childhood that is structured, schedules, and screen based. Lo’ak’s laughter carries a weight that goes beyond nostalgia, as it pictures what embodies freedom (UD9) actually looks like.



Belonging as unconditional

One of the most powerful moments in these fanfictions comes from A Spider and the Complex where there’s a quiet reflection of what community actually means in the Na’vi culture.


A Spider and the Complex — Ch. 1

"Sometimes being a part of a community is more than just being accepted. It's about being able to be an inconvenience or annoying without ending up excommunicated. Here, he could put himself first when he needed to, and people would fit themselves into his schedule just as much as he did theirs." Belonging with no strings attached.
EB2
Extending Beyond the Film: Fan shows internal feelings, emotional connections, awe, grief, or joy not represented in the film either by the characters in the films or the fans of the films.
UD1
Utopian Desire: Desire to feel part of a safe, welcoming and supportive social space.
UD2
Utopian Desire: Desire for comfort, nurture, protection and emotional well-being within a community.
UD5
Utopian Desire: Desire for learning, teaching and performing meaningful roles within a community rather than meaningless work.


This is a powerful line. This fan author is describing an unconditional community where your place is not reliant on your performance or productivity. The way this is stated reveals everything that is lacking in the world fans actually live in. There is no dramatic gesture of acceptance but rather something quieter and rarer where you can be messy or bothersome and still belong. The utopian desire (UD1) is not something fantastical; it is something that should be ordinary in the real world.



This unconditional belonging and care is felt in Pandora, but also enacted carefully in the small gestures. In A Spider and the Complex, the fan author expands the traumatic conflict between Neytiri and Spider that occurred in the second film (For those that don’t know - Neytiri tried to kill Spider - long story…). This was something that was left unresolved in the film.


A Spider and the Complex — Ch. 1

"Their past was a complicated one, so Neytiri made a conscious choice to always be calm around him, never move too quickly… Whenever he entered their home, she'd place down any blades she happened to be holding, no matter the task she was in the middle of. She'd stop carving wood, choosing instead to spend time with him. She'd quit chopping fruit for dinner, leaving the knife available so he could pick up the task." Care as a series of quiet, deliberate choices.

EB2
Extending Beyond the Film: Fan shows internal feelings, emotional connections, awe, grief, or joy not represented in the film either by the characters in the films or the fans of the films.
UD1
Utopian Desire: Desire to feel part of a safe, welcoming and supportive social space.
UD2
Utopian Desire: Desire for comfort, nurture, protection and emotional well-being within a community.
UD10
Utopian Desire: Desire for a community where mourning and/or emotional processing are experienced collectively.


With the second film leaving Neytiri’s and Spider’s relationship broken and unresolved, this fan author attempts to fix it through small everyday gestures from Neytiri, as she ‘sets down a blade, leaves a task unfinished, creates space for Spider.’ EB7 is important here as the fan is filling a story gap by showing what repair really looks like in Pandora. Each gesture is a performance of belonging (UD2), creating a space filled with recognition and care. As Duffett (2013) notes, fans form emotional attachments to stories as a way to construct identity and feel belonging. In this fanfiction, those feelings of care and belonging are not just consumed, they are written into being through Neytiri’s actions.

Through EB4, this fan author extends the film’s storyline by showing how the Na’vi relationship with animals is taught and understood as mutual not hierarchical. There is no ownership over animals, there is only kinship. UD4 (Spiritual Continuity) operates here through the way this value is being transmitted through parent to child as a part of what it means to live well. This is a stark contrast that is left unsaid but unavoidable to how animals are treated as resources, commodities, and entertainment, in fans' real world.



This value is not only shown as a lesson but also as something children get to experience.

The trembling hands, the hesitation, the body jolting.. This fan author explores the deep emotional and physical aspects that Na’ci go through when bonding with an animal, which is not fully explored in the film. EB2 here is doing the work as these descriptions show someone carefully crossing an important and sacred moment. In the film, audiences can see the Na’vi bond with animals, but this scene adds an important interpretation that shows how intense and emotional this moment really is. What the fan is building through UD4 in this scene is a world where children are taught to connect deeply with other living beings, and to actually feel what another creature feels. The trembling and hesitation shows that this connection isn't easy and that it takes effort, but that’s exactly what makes it meaningful.

Fans are imagining a world that holds grief in a meaningfully different way.

The destruction of the ‘Sea Dragon’ in the second film is a symbol of violence and loss, which is then reimagined by the fan author, as Tsireya collects sea glass from the Sea Dragon wreckage and turns it into something beautiful for Lo’ak to wear. UD8 here is important as EB4 works here through Tsierya’s collecting of seaglass from the wreckage which suggests UD8 where a new Metkayina practice is explored in which beauty is found in the aftermath of destruction and violence. This reflects a deep cultural relationship with the sea as a provider and memory keeper, even through destruction suggesting resilience and continuity of meaning.



Result

Looking at these two fanfictions together, they are not merely just escapism, but rather a diagnosis of what fans see missing in the real world. The details fans add and the scenes they tend to linger on all point to the things missing from the world fans live in, such as slowness, unconditional belonging, meaningful or purposeful work, grief that is supported instead of hidden, and ethical and respectful relationships with nature.

Zuolo (2025) points to the way utopias are not meant to be perfect plans for society, but as “indirect critiques of the contemporary status quo.” They help us look at our own world from a different lens through estrangement. Jameson (2010) describes utopian desire as “detective work” which reads the real world for clues about what would make it better, and move toward it. In Avatar fanfiction, this detective work is happening one scene at a time, through the weaving, the fire, the body that finally gets to slump, and in the seaglass that once was wreckage. This is what Jenkins (2003) participatory culture looks like. It is more about the quiet, careful construction of a world that knows exactly what ours is missing, and refuses to stop imagining it anyway. The world these fanfictions are diagnosing a world of climate crisis, industrial farming, extractive colonialism, and social atomisation. A world where the things Pandora offers have not been lost by accident but removed by design.
