Overview

Rather than asking Avatar fans directly what Pandora means to them, this project watched what they made, what they wrote, what they taught each other, and what they grieved, and read those practices as evidence of something larger. What follows is an explanation of how that evidence was gathered, how it was analysed, and what the analytical frameworks mean. If you want the full technical detail, the codebook is available at the bottom of this page.



On scale and the nature of the study

This project can be considered a qualitative pilot study, instead of a large quantitative study. Two stories were used to analyse fanfiction, language community data was collected over two platforms, and selected TikTok and Reddit content was based on total numbers of engagements. The number of data points coded across the three areas was 78 passages/posts, which is enough to identify trends, but not enough to make generalizable statistical claims about the Avatar fandom as a whole. This is the first phase of a larger research project. This will create a map to the research that will serve to develop larger studies in the future.



On coding objectivity and verification

The coding framework was generated using data and implemented in a systematic way across all three datasets. To limit subjectivity in the process, each segment of text was coded according to explicit criteria defined in the codebook, and codes were used at either the scene or post level, as opposed to impressionistically. The total count of codes confirms that valid patterns are established by UDI (31) and UD2 (29) and EB2 (25 instances) throughout the data set. However, every code assigned in qualitative research carries an element of interpretation and hence readers are free to examine and question the process behind code assignments through an open access codebook.



Fanfiction: how it was selected and coded

The fanfiction analysis in Constructing Utopia draws on two fan-written stories found on Archive of Our Own, the largest fanfiction platform on the internet. These were So Somewhere in My Childhood and A Spider and the Complex. Both were selected because they are representative of a specific and common genre within Avatar fandom, which is quiet, domestic, slice-of-life stories that expand the world of Pandora through ordinary moments rather than dramatic plot.

Each story was read closely and coded scene by scene. Coding is a research method that involves systematically tagging passages of text according to a set of categories. In this case, three frameworks were developed specifically for this project. What a fan is doing creatively (EB codes), what they are longing for (UD codes), and what kind of fan practice the scene represents (FP codes). The goal of coding is not to reduce a story to a spreadsheet but to make patterns visible and to show, across many scenes and many fanfictions, what Avatar fans consistently return to and why.



Na'vi language community: how it was collected

The language community data in Speaking into Being was collected from two online platforms. Kelutral.org and LearnNavi.org were the two platforms analyzed. These were open communities, welcoming anyone interested in learning, teaching, or simply exploring the Na'vi language.

The data was gathered by observing public posts, channels, and forum discussions from January to March 2026. The selection of posts was designed to represent the range of activities on each platform, which included grammar questions and their answers, naming conventions, community events, and discussions conducted entirely in Na'vi.



TikTok and Reddit: how it was collected

The affective data in Feeling Utopia was collected from two platforms. These were TikTok and Reddit posts. On TikTok, videos were found using search terms including "Pandora depression," "I want to live on Pandora," and "I want to be Na'vi." On Reddit, I gathered posts and comment threads from r/Avatar, with the search focused on terms like "Avatar depression," "post-Avatar depression," "Avatar blues," and "I want to live on Pandora."

High-engagement (i.e., those that have the most likes, and/or comments) posts were good indicators of what content had the biggest impact on users more broadly rather than isolated individuals' thoughts and experiences. The top comment for each post was recorded alongside the post itself in order to better represent how the community as a whole responded to these engagements.



Sentiment analysis and the Star Trek comparison

To understand what makes Avatar's utopian imagination specific rather than just typical of science fiction fandom generally, this project compared Avatar fan discourse with Star Trek fan discourse using sentiment analysis.

Sentiment analysis is a computational method that reads text and scores it for emotional tone. This project used Python's sentiment analysis tools in Google Colab to score posts from r/Avatar and r/StarTrek with emotional tones, such as sadness, joy, optimism, approval, desire, and disappointment. The results show not just what each fandom feels, but the emotional structure of how they relate to the gap between their desired world and the world they live in.

Sentiment analysis is good at detecting patterns across large amounts of text, but is not good at understanding context or nuance. For this reason, the sentiment results in this project are treated as indicators rather than conclusions, and the quantitative results are always read alongside the qualitative analysis of specific posts and their contexts.



Ethical considerations

The project acknowledges that as a fan and researcher, I am not a neutral observer of this material, as I share the emotional experiences I am analysing. This positionality can be a strength, as it gives me access to the emotional logic of the fandom from the inside, but it also requires transparency. This is why my own position as a fan is stated clearly in the introduction and acknowledged throughout.

This project also takes seriously the fact that some of the data - particularly the Reddit posts in the Feeling Utopia section - involves real people expressing genuine grief, distress, and vulnerability in public spaces. Several considerations shaped how this data was handled.

All data used is publicly posted and publicly accessible. No private or restricted content was accessed at any point. All usernames have been removed from quoted material, and quotes are used to illustrate broader patterns rather than to analyse or evaluate individual people. The project does not make clinical or diagnostic claims about any individual's mental health based on their posts.



The codebook

Three coding frameworks were developed for this project. Each framework captures a different dimension of what Avatar fans are doing when they engage with Pandora.

EB codes (Extending Beyond Film) track what fans are doing creatively, such as how they expand, invent, or repair Pandora's world beyond what the films show. UD codes (Utopian Desire) track what fans are longing for, such as the specific values and ways of living that feel missing from the real world. FP codes (Fan Practice) track the type of creative or social activity the data represents.
