Introduction

I was five years old when Avatar first came out, and my sister was nine. We walked out of the cinema and into our car parked in the middle of winter and neither of us said anything for quite some time.

Then we went home and became Navi.

For years after, we played around the house together with bow and arrows bought from the dollar store too many times to count, and turned whatever space we had into Pandora through nothing but our own imagination. We played the original Avatar game on Xbox, side by side, for hours. When the news came that there would be a sequel, we were the first to call each other, and when the game Avatar: Frontiers of Pandora came out, we were back playing together, both in our twenties, still returning to the same world we had found when we were children.

17 years and we never stopped going back.

I used to think that this passion we felt was just what all people felt over their favourite film, but the more I looked into it, the more I realised that millions of people around the world felt the same thing. They grieved for Pandora and kept returning to it just as we had. There is even a name for this feeling that occurs when the credit roll, which is called Post-Pandora Depression or Post-Avatar Depression Syndrome. It’s described as the grief of having to leave a fictional world that is more real, livable, and more right than the one you actually live in.

This led me to a question that this project, Longing for Pandora, takes seriously: why does a fictional moon feel more like home than the planet we were born on?

Longing for Pandora argues that Avatar fans are not simply escaping into fantasy, but they are doing something more specific and significant. They are using creative practices to construct Pandora as an ecological utopia that names, with precision, what is missing from modern life.
